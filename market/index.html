Stocks

Stocks are shares of ownership in a company, allowing holders to benefit from its growth and profits. They are traded on markets where prices fluctuate based on supply, demand, and company performance.

Options

Unlike stocks, which represent actual ownership in a company, options are derivative contracts. This means they get their value from something else — usually a stock. An option gives the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell a stock at a specific price (called the strike price) before or on a certain date (the expiration date). However, in this simulation, options are cash-settled i.e. no one actually receives shares.

Market

A market is a system where buyers and sellers interact to trade financial assets like stocks or options. All trades happen through a system of orders, and prices change based on what people are willing to pay or accept. Markets work through order books, which keep track of who wants to buy and sell and at what prices.

Orderbook, Bids, Asks

The order book is a real-time list of all buy and sell orders for a specific stock or option. It’s how the system keeps track of supply and demand.

  • Bid: The price someone is currently willing to pay to buy.
  • Ask (or Offer): The price someone is willing to accept to sell.
  • Spread: The difference between the best bid and best ask (highest bid and lowest ask).

    When a buyer and seller agree on a price, a trade happens, and the order book is updated.

Options Chain

An options chain is a table that shows all available options for a stock, organized by:

  • Strike price (the price you can buy or sell the stock for)
  • Expiration date (when the option contract ends)
  • Type: Call (right to buy) or Put (right to sell)
