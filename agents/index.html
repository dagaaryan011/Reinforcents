In our market simulation environment, we model three primary types of traders to reflect realistic market dynamics. Each trader type represents a unique role and behavior pattern.

1. Retail Trader

Retail traders are individual investors or small traders who participate with relatively small capital and limited technical resources. Their decisions are often influenced by market sentiment, news, or speculative motives.

  • Trade small amounts, often on popular or cheap options.
  • Buy options expecting big moves, but may not fully understand the risks.
  • Act emotionally — buying when prices rise, selling when they fall.

2. Market Maker

Market makers are the backbone of options market liquidity. They continuously quote bid and ask prices for multiple strikes and expirations, profiting from the bid-ask spread while managing significant inventory and risk.

  • Constantly post prices to buy and sell options.
  • Make money from the difference between the buy and sell price (the “spread”).
  • Adjust prices based on supply, demand, and their own inventory.

3 Institutional Trader

Institutional traders represent large-scale participants such as hedge funds, pension funds, or investment banks. They typically execute large orders with sophisticated strategies and access to significant resources.

  • Trade in large sizes, often across many different options at once.
  • Focus on long-term strategies instead of quick profits.
  • Use tools and data to make decisions and reduce risk.
